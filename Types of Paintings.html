Warli Art

Warli painting







Warli art is a form of tribal art and it belongs to the warlis in the state of Maharashtra. It has been practiced for over 2500 years. They showcase daily activities of the local people of the community like hunting, praying, farming, and dancing and the elements of nature. These paintings have a primary focus on representing social and daily life. They are closely connected to nature and not mythlogical charecters or deities. Warli paintings are traditionally made on mud walls of huts using rice paste and twigs.

The paintings consist of white colored geometric designs and patterns like triangles, squares, and circles. Mountains and trees are represented by a triangle, humans by squares. The sun and moon are represented by circles, and these circular patterns also represent their belief that death is just another beggining. The primary motif of a Warli painting is the mother goddess, Palaghat. They have customs in which a marrige cannot take place without the painting that represents Palaghat. As time evolved, these paintings are also done on canvas, paper, and cloth. Jivya Soma Mashe, a renowned Indian artist, in particular a legendary Wareli artist. He was awarded the padma Shri in 2011 for his enormous contribution towards Warli painting.

Kalamkari Painting







The name "Kalamkari" painting is derived from the words "Kalam" which means pen in hndi and "Kari" whhich means craftsmanship. The Kalamkari art form is practiced by many families and people in Andhra Pradesh "a state in India" and some villages in Tamil Nadu. This painting form usually depicts scenes from epic battles and legendary stories such as Ramayana and Mahabharata, musical instruments, animals, Bhuddhist art, flowers and Hindu symbols like swastika. Kalamkari art is an organic hand printing or block printing art from which is practiced for over 3000 years. The painting is done using a pen or a bamboo reed on fabric and natural earthy colors and vegetable dies are used, including colors like indigo, gree, brown, black, and mustard.

It is generally made on cotton fabric and it involves 20 different steps. The art form is widely used on sarees (a traditional clothing for woman in India) and other ethinic wear. The painting is done using a Kalam or a bamboo reed on fabric and natural and earthy colors and vegetable dyes are used like indigo, green, rust, black, and mustard. It is generally done on cotton fabric and it involves a twenty-three steps process. The Kalamkari art form is widely used on sarees and ethnic wear and is very popular.

Phad Painting







This type of Indian folk painting is practiced in the state of Rajasthan, India. It is a religious scroll painting style and traditionally done on along peice of cloth or canvas which is also known as the Phad (as the name suggests). Scroll painting is very common in Asian traditions, distinguishing between: Handscroll, which is a painting in horizontal format. Hanging scroll, is a painting in vertical format. Phads consist of fold deities of Rajasthan, mainly Pabuju and Devnarayan. The Bhopas, preists of the fold deities in Rajasthan carry and perform in front of a Phad using it in the form of a portable temple. It is a narrative scroll painting tradition on which stories fo their deities and heroes like Prithviraj Chauhan are painting in the colors of orange, red and yellow.

Phad paintigns usually depict battlefeilds, adventures, etc. The paintings are highly detailed and artists accomadate many difdferent stories at once through a single peice of art canvas. The Joshi community extensively practiced the Phad art from Shree Lal Joshi, a world renowned Phad artis, and a Padma Shree as well as Shilguru award winner. This both rewards are highly prestigous and are awared to the legends of painting styles. Lal Joshi preserved and modernized this style, and opened a school "Chirtrashala" for everyone to learn, and carry the art style for future generations.

Madhubani Painting







The Madhubani paintings or Mithila paintings originated in the Madhubani village in Bihar and are mostly created by women. This style of Indian painting requires many tools like fingers, brushes, pens, matchsticks, twigs, and natural colors. They are made on walls, floors of holy places, canvases, etc. The Madhubani paintings consist of bright colors which are characterized by their geometrical patterns and are widely practiced in the Madhubani district in Bihar. Originally, these paintings were made on mud walls and soil ground or floors. Now, they are also made on canvases, cloth, and handmade paper. It is one of the most celebrated and popular styles of folk painting in India. The themes/scenes of Madhubani paintings are mostly that from nature, such as sun, moon, tulsi plants and scenes from mythology and Hindu deities like Krishna, Rama, Shiva, Durga, Lakshmi, Saraswati, etc. They also include court scenes, social events, and wedding scenes. The origin of this art form was during the Ramayana period when Raja Janak (Sita’s father) requested the villagers to decorate the whole village on the occasion of Prince Rama’s and Sita’s wedding. Many Madhubani artists received several awards such as the National award and Padma Shri making the paintings style so popular and widely practiced across India.

Mandana Art







The Mandana art form is is used for decorations, and is a tribal, and one of the most oldest painting styles to exist in India. It is drawn on the walls and floors of houses in Rajasthan and Madhya Pradesh (states in India) as a way to protect their home and fireplace. Mandanas are made to welcome gods and also on festive occasions. The women of the Meena community in Rajasthan and Madhya Pradesh are the first Mandana artists. The Mandana paintings are very simple yet eye-catching. These paintings consist of cotton, twigs, a bunch of squirrel hair, and a brush made from a date stick. The base is prepared using cow dung mixed with rati (local clay), water, and red ochre. For making the motif, lime, brick, and chalk powder are used. Some basic designs of Mandana paintings are peacocks, tigers, floral, women working, Lord Ganesha, geometrical shapes, jaali, and Vedic yagna. The art form is dying and needs to find people who can keep it alive and help it regain its old popularity. This art form has later evolved, and been adapted by other parts of the world, asnd therefore the glimpse of the Mandana desings are highly visible in the famous rangolis which are designs made from powedered color during Diwali.
